2. Executive Summary

Swedish employment law governs the rights and obligations arising in the relationship between employers and employees in Sweden. It covers employment protection, co-determination, working time, annual leave, work-environment duties, labour disputes, parental leave, collective-agreement interaction and cross-border work situations where labour is performed in Sweden.

The field is characterised by the interaction between statute and collective bargaining. For that reason, a Swedish employment-law page should not present legislation in isolation, but should instead connect statutes, institutional actors, supervision, workplace processes and dispute pathways into one coherent legal framework.

11. Authorities

The Government Offices of Sweden maintain the official policy area for labour law and work environment and publish a labour-legislation page containing English translations of several labour statutes. Those translations are expressly described as non-official and for general information, which means the authentic statutory texts remain the Swedish legal texts in Swedish.

The Swedish Labour Court is the special court set up to hear and rule on labour-related disputes. The Swedish Work Environment Authority provides guidance on work-environment law and states that the Working Hours Act regulates how much one may work per day, per week and per year, including breaks, on-call time and night rest.

12. Legislation

The Government Offices’ labour-legislation collection identifies a core body of labour statutes relevant to Swedish employment law, including the Employment Protection Act, the Employment (Co-Determination in the Workplace) Act, the Annual Leave Act, the Work Environment Act, the Working Hours Act, the Parental Leave Act, the Posting of Workers Act, the Agency Work Act and labour-dispute procedure legislation.

21. Cross-Border Relevance

Cross-border relevance is substantial in Swedish employment law because foreign employers, temporary work in Sweden and worker-posting situations all trigger legal questions. The Swedish Work Environment Authority states that Swedish work-environment regulations apply to everyone working in Sweden, even if the work is temporary or the employer is registered in another country.

The authority also explains that the Working Hours Act applies, with certain exceptions, to all work carried out in Sweden and that this applies regardless of whether the employer is Swedish or foreign, provided that the activities are carried out in Sweden. This makes cross-border compliance a core part of Employment Law Sweden rather than a peripheral section.
